## Chapter 1: The Rise of Nationalism in Europe

### Key Concepts
- **Nation-state**: a state where citizens share a sense of common identity and history
- **French Revolution (1789)**: introduced ideas of la patrie (fatherland), le citoyen (citizen), unified laws, common language
- **Napoleon's role**: conquered Europe, spread nationalism, but also created opposition (reactionary nationalism)
- **Congress of Vienna (1815)**: conservative regimes restored monarchy; Metternich as host
- **Liberalism**: freedom of press, individual rights, end of privileges, free market
- **Giuseppe Mazzini**: Young Italy, Young Europe — inspired revolutionary movements
- **Unification of Italy**: Mazzini (ideals), Cavour (diplomacy), Garibaldi (military)
- **Unification of Germany**: Otto von Bismarck, "blood and iron" policy, three wars (Danish, Austrian, Franco-Prussian), Kaiser William I proclaimed Emperor at Versailles (1871)
- **Romanticism**: cultural nationalism through art, poetry, folklore (Grimm brothers, Karol Kurpinski)
- **Greek War of Independence (1821-1832)**: Lord Byron's support, Treaty of Constantinople

### Important Dates
```
1789 — French Revolution begins
1804 — Napoleon's Civil Code
1815 — Congress of Vienna, Treaty of Vienna
1821 — Greek War of Independence begins
1831 — Mazzini founds Young Italy
1848 — Revolutions across Europe (liberal middle class)
1859-1861 — Unification of Italy
1866-1871 — Unification of Germany
1871 — German Empire proclaimed at Versailles
```

### Key Figures
| Person | Role |
|--------|------|
| Napoleon Bonaparte | Spread revolutionary ideas through conquests |
| Duke Metternich | Hosted Congress of Vienna, conservative |
| Giuseppe Mazzini | Founded Young Italy, republican ideals |
| Count Cavour | Chief Minister of Sardinia-Piedmont, diplomacy |
| Giuseppe Garibaldi | Led Red Shirts, unified southern Italy |
| Otto von Bismarck | Chancellor of Prussia, unified Germany |
| Kaiser William I | First German Emperor |
| Lord Byron | English poet, supported Greek independence |

### Common Exam Traps
- Confusing Mazzini (ideals/vision) with Cavour (diplomacy) and Garibaldi (military action)
- Not knowing the exact year of German unification (1871)
- Forgetting that Napoleon both spread AND created opposition to nationalism

## Chapter 2: Nationalism in India

### Key Concepts
- **First World War & Indian response**: war created economic hardship, inspired self-rule demands
- **Rowlatt Act (1919)**: British could imprison without trial; Gandhi called it unjust
- **Jallianwala Bagh Massacre (April 13, 1919)**: General Dyer, Amritsar; national outrage
- **Non-Cooperation Movement (1920-22)**:
  - Reasons: Khilafat issue + Jallianwala Bagh + Rowlatt Act
  - Methods: boycott of schools, courts, foreign cloth, councils; hand spinning (khadi)
  - Ended: Chauri Chaura incident (Feb 1922) — Gandhi withdrew
- **Simon Commission (1928)**: all-British, no Indian members; widespread protests
- **Salt March / Dandi March (March 12, 1930)**: Gandhi walked 240 miles to Dandi; broke salt law
- **Civil Disobedience Movement (1930-34)**:
  - Methods: breaking salt law, boycott of foreign goods, no-tax campaigns
  - Gandhi-Irwin Pact (March 1931)
  - Second Round Table Conference (London)
- **Quit India Movement (1942)**: "Do or Die" slogan
- **Role of different social groups**: business class, peasants, tribals, women, workers

### Important Dates
```
1915 — Gandhi returns from South Africa
1919 — Rowlatt Act; Jallianwala Bagh massacre (April 13)
1920 — Non-Cooperation Movement launched (Nagpur session)
1922 — Chauri Chaura incident; movement withdrawn
1927 — Simon Commission appointed
1928 — Simon Commission arrives in India
1929 — Lahore Congress session: Purna Swaraj demand
1930 — Dandi March (March 12); Civil Disobedience begins
1931 — Gandhi-Irwin Pact (March 5)
1935 — Government of India Act
1942 — Quit India Movement (August 8)
1947 — Indian Independence (August 15)
```

### Map Work (CRITICAL — free marks)
```
MUST KNOW locations on India map:
1. Indian National Congress Sessions: Calcutta (1920), Nagpur (1920), Madras (1927)
2. Important centres of Non-Cooperation: Kheda, Champaran, Ahmedabad, Chauri Chaura
3. Important centres of Civil Disobedience: Dandi, Dharasana
4. Jallianwala Bagh — Amritsar, Punjab
```

### Common Exam Traps
- Mixing up Non-Cooperation Movement (1920-22) with Civil Disobedience Movement (1930-34)
- Not knowing why Gandhi withdrew Non-Cooperation (Chauri Chaura violence)
- Forgetting the Khilafat connection to Non-Cooperation
- Not mentioning the role of women (Sarojini Naidu at Dharasana)

## Chapter 3: The Making of a Global World

### Key Concepts
- **Silk Routes**: ancient trade links (silk, spices, textiles, Buddhism)
- **Pre-modern world**: trade in food, culture, and disease (Smallpox from Americas)
- **Colonialism**: Europeans exploited resources, labor, markets of colonized countries
- **Indentured labor**: harsh contract system, Indian workers to Caribbean, Fiji, Mauritius
- **Great Depression (1930s)**:
  - Started with USA stock market crash (1929)
  - Overproduction, falling prices, bank failures
  - Impact on India: agricultural prices crashed, peasants suffered
- **Bretton Woods Conference (1944)**: created IMF and World Bank
- **Post-WWII**: decolonization, new economic order, globalization
- **MNCs (Multinational Corporations)**: global production, spread of technology

### Important Dates
```
1885 — Big European migration era
1929 — Wall Street Crash, Great Depression begins
1944 — Bretton Woods Conference (IMF, World Bank)
1945 — WWII ends
1947 — GATT established
```

## Chapter 4: The Age of Industrialisation

### Key Concepts
- **Proto-industrialisation**: production in countryside before factories
- **Industrial Revolution in Britain**: cotton textiles, iron, steam engine
- **Industrialisation in India**:
  - Decline of Indian handloom (British machine-made goods caused decline)
  - Early entrepreneurs: Dwarkanath Tagore, Jamsetjee Nusserwanjee Tata, Seth Hukumchand
  - Bombay and Calcutta as industrial centres
  - First World War: Indian factories produce for war → growth of Indian industry
- **Impact on workers**: low wages, poor conditions, seasonal employment, no job security
- **Technology**: spinning jenny, steam engine; BUT hand labor didn't disappear completely

### Key Facts
```
1730s — Earliest factories in England
1764 — Spinning Jenny (James Hargreaves)
1769 — Steam engine (James Watt)
1854 — First cotton mill in Bombay
1855 — First jute mill in Calcutta
1907 — TISCO (Tata Iron and Steel Company) in Jamshedpur
```

## Chapter 5: Print Culture and the Modern World

### Key Concepts
- **Earliest print technology**: China (594 AD), Japan (Buddhist Diamond Sutra, 868 AD)
- **Gutenberg's printing press (1430s)**: movable type, Bible first major book
- **Impact of print**: spread of ideas, Reformation (Martin Luther 95 Theses), science, literacy
- **Print in India**:
  - Portuguese missionaries (Goa, 1550s)
  - First Indian newspaper: Bengal Gazette (1780, James Hickey)
  - Raja Rammohun Roy: Sambad Kaumudi, social reform through press
  - Vernacular Press Act (1878): restricted Indian language newspapers
  - Print fueled nationalism: Bal Gangadhar Tilak (Kesari), social reformers used print

### Key Facts
```
594 AD — First known print in China
868 AD — Diamond Sutra (oldest Japanese printed book)
1430s — Gutenberg's press (Germany)
1517 — Luther's 95 Theses (Reformation)
1780 — Bengal Gazette (first Indian newspaper)
1878 — Vernacular Press Act
```

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
1. Not writing exact dates and years (examiners check for precision)
2. Mixing up European and Indian nationalism timelines
3. Not linking movements to their causes and consequences
4. Poor map marking — use correct symbols, mark precisely
5. Writing long, unfocused answers instead of point-wise with dates

## Answer Writing Framework for History
1. **Use dates in EVERY answer** — "In 1919, the Rowlatt Act was passed..."
2. **Name the people** — Gandhi, Nehru, Bismarck, Mazzini — examiners look for names
3. **Cause-Event-Consequence** format for explaining movements
4. **For 5M questions**: Write 5-6 points with sub-explanations
5. **Map answers**: Mark precisely, write the name NEXT to the mark, not far away
6. **Use source-based answers**: "According to the text..." when applicable
