# ⚡ Ch02 - Electrostatic Potential and Capacitance

> **Physics** | **Marks**: ~8 | [[🔬 Physics]]
> This chapter covers potential energy of charges, the concept of Electrostatic Potential, and the storage of charge in Capacitors.

---

## 🎯 Conceptual Core (NCERT Mirror)

### 1. Electrostatic Potential ($V$)
- Work done per unit charge in bringing it from infinity: $V = \frac{W}{q_0}$.
- **Point Charge Potential**: $V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{r}$.
- **Potential Difference**: $V_B - V_A = \frac{W_{AB}}{q}$.

### 2. Equipotential Surfaces
- Surfaces where potential is constant everywhere.
- **Work Done** on moving a charge along an equipotential surface is **Zero**.
- Electric field lines are always **perpendicular** to equipotential surfaces.

### 3. Electrostatic Potential Energy ($U$)
- For a system of two charges: $U = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q_1 q_2}{r}$.
- For a dipole in uniform field: $U = -pE \cos \theta = -\vec{p} \cdot \vec{E}$.

---

## 📐 The 12th Grade Technicals

### ⚡ Mandatory Derivations
1. **Potential due to Dipole**: $V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{p \cos \theta}{r^2}$. (Zero on equatorial plane).
2. **Capacitance of Parallel Plate Capacitor**: $C = \frac{\epsilon_0 A}{d}$.
3. **Energy Stored in Capacitor**: $U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{Q^2}{2C} = \frac{1}{2}QV$.

### 🔋 Capacitance & Dielectrics
- **Dielectric Effect**: When a dielectric of constant $K$ is inserted:
  - $C' = KC$
  - If battery disconnected: $Q$ constant, $V$ decreases, $E$ decreases.
  - If battery connected: $V$ constant, $Q$ increases, $E$ constant.
- **Capacitors in Series**: $\frac{1}{C_s} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \dots$
- **Capacitors in Parallel**: $C_p = C_1 + C_2 + \dots$

---

## ⚠️ The "Mistake DNA" Alert (Common Traps)
- **Sign of Work**: Distinguish between work done **by** the field and work done **by external force**.
- **Equatorial Potential**: Potential of a dipole on the equatorial line is **Always Zero**, but the Field is NOT zero.
- **Energy Density**: $u = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 E^2$. Don't confuse it with total energy.
