# ⚡ Ch01 - Electric Charges and Fields

> **Physics** | **Marks**: ~8 | [[🔬 Physics]]
> This chapter introduces the concept of electric charge and the forces between them (Coulomb's Law), leading to the abstraction of the Electric Field and Gauss's Law.

---

## 🎯 Conceptual Core (NCERT Mirror)

### 1. Properties of Electric Charge
- **Quantization**: $q = \pm ne$, where $e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} C$.
- **Conservation**: Total charge of an isolated system remains constant.
- **Additivity**: Charges add up like real numbers.

### 2. Coulomb's Law
- Force between two point charges: $F = k \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$ where $k = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \approx 9 \times 10^9 \, N m^2 C^{-2}$.
- **Principle of Superposition**: Net force is the vector sum of individual forces.

### 3. Electric Field ($\vec{E}$)
- Force per unit test charge: $\vec{E} = \frac{\vec{F}}{q_0}$.
- **Field of a Point Charge**: $E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{r^2}$.
- **Electric Dipole**: Two equal and opposite charges separated by $2a$. Dipole moment $\vec{p} = q(2a)$.

---

## 📐 The 12th Grade Technicals

### ⚡ Mandatory Derivations
1. **Field of a Dipole (Axial)**: $E_{axial} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{2pr}{(r^2-a^2)^2} \approx \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{2p}{r^3}$.
2. **Field of a Dipole (Equatorial)**: $E_{equi} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{p}{(r^2+a^2)^{3/2}} \approx \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{p}{r^3}$.
3. **Torque on Dipole in Uniform Field**: $\tau = pE \sin \theta \implies \vec{\tau} = \vec{p} \times \vec{E}$.

### 🌐 Gauss's Law & Applications
- **Statement**: Total flux $\phi = \oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{q_{enclosed}}{\epsilon_0}$.
- **Applications (Mandatory)**:
  - Field due to **Infinite Line Charge**: $E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\epsilon_0 r}$.
  - Field due to **Infinite Plane Sheet**: $E = \frac{\sigma}{2\epsilon_0}$.
  - Field due to **Thin Spherical Shell**: Inside $E=0$, Outside $E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{r^2}$.

---

## ⚠️ The "Mistake DNA" Alert (Common Traps)
- **Vector Summation**: Students often add field magnitudes scalar-wise. **Always use vector components**.
- **$\epsilon_0$ vs $\epsilon$**: Forgetting the dielectric constant $K$ when charges are in a medium ($F_{med} = F_{vac}/K$).
- **Equatorial Field Direction**: The field is **opposite** to the dipole moment vector.
